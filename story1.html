Lost in Translation

During school holidays, 7-year-old Themba Mkhize tagged along with his mom to her job as a domestic worker. While she cleaned the massive house, Themba entertained himself with Rudy, the white kid she watched.

This house was unlike any Themba had seen. The backyard boasted a theme park, complete with swings, slides, and merry-go-rounds. Themba thought, "What is this, Disneyland for one?"

Rudy, eager to play, approached Themba. "Can you give me a swing?" she asked.

Themba's English vocabulary consisted of just two words: "yes" and "no." He hesitated, unsure which was the right answer.

"Can you give me a swing?" Rudy repeated.

Themba replied, "Yes."

Five seconds later, Rudy asked again, "Can you give me a swing?"

This time, Themba said, "No."

Their exchange became a comedic tennis match: "Yes." "No." "Yes." "No." Rudy's hopes and dreams were the ball, and Themba was unintentionally smashing them straight into the net.

Rudy burst into tears, and Themba stood bewildered. "Why is she crying? I'm answering all her questions!" he thought.

Years passed, and Themba learned to speak English fluently. He mastered effective communication and gained confidence in his responses.

Looking back, Themba chuckled. "It's not just about language; it's about growth." He imagined Rudy's pride if she saw him now.

Perhaps Rudy would still cry, but this time, Themba could explain why.
